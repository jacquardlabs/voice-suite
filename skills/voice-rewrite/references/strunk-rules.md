# Craft Rules — condensed from Strunk, *The Elements of Style* (1918)

> Provenance: condensed from the public-domain 1918 text by William Strunk Jr.
> (Project Gutenberg #37134). Rule numbering and selected examples follow the
> original. This is the craft layer for the voice suite — it is bundled here so
> no external skill or network fetch is ever required.

## How to apply these (read first)

1. **These rules are the craft floor, not the voice.** The user's voice profile
   is *descriptive* (how they actually write); these rules are *prescriptive*
   defaults. Wherever the profile records an observed trait, anti-pattern, or
   explicit Strunk exemption, **the profile wins**. Never "correct" the user's
   voice into Strunk's.
2. **Structural rules nearly always apply** (Rules 1–9, 16–17: grammar,
   punctuation, paragraph discipline, word order). Nobody's authentic voice is
   comma splices and dangling participles.
3. **Voice-adjacent rules yield to observed traits** (Rules 10–15, 18: active
   voice, positive form, concision, sentence rhythm, emphasis). Apply them only
   where the profile is silent or marks low coverage.
4. **Concision is not terseness.** Strunk's own caveat: this requires "not that
   the writer make all his sentences short... but that he make every word
   tell." Cutting needless words is not the same as flattening rhythm.

## Grammar & punctuation (Rules 1–7)

**Rule 1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's.**
Charles's friend; Burns's poems — regardless of the final consonant.

**Rule 2. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a
comma after each term except the last.**
red, white, and blue — the serial comma stays.

**Rule 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas.**
Both commas or neither: "His brother, you will be pleased to hear, is now in
perfect health."

**Rule 4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing a co-ordinate
clause.** "The situation is perilous, but there is still one chance of escape."

**Rule 5. Do not join independent clauses by a comma.**
✗ It is nearly half past five, we cannot reach town before dark.
✓ It is nearly half past five; we cannot reach town before dark. (Or two
sentences.)

**Rule 6. Do not break sentences in two.**
A fragment posing as a sentence usually belongs to its neighbor:
✗ He was an interesting talker. A man who had traveled all over the world.
✓ He was an interesting talker, a man who had traveled all over the world.
(Exemptable: some writers fragment deliberately for rhythm — check the
profile.)

**Rule 7. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to
the grammatical subject.**
✗ On arriving in Chicago, his friends met him at the station.
✓ When he arrived in Chicago, his friends met him at the station.

## Composition (Rules 8–18)

**Rule 8. Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each
topic.**

**Rule 9. Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity
with the beginning.** The reader should be able to skim openers and follow the
argument.

**Rule 10. Use the active voice.** *(voice-adjacent)*
✗ There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground.
✓ Dead leaves covered the ground.
Caveat from the original: passive is right when the paragraph's topic demands
it as subject ("The dramatists of the Restoration are little esteemed to-day"
is correct in a paragraph *about the dramatists*). Avoid one passive depending
on another, and avoid nouns that swallow the action:
✗ A survey of this region was made in 1900. → ✓ This region was surveyed in
1900.

**Rule 11. Put statements in positive form.** *(voice-adjacent)*
✗ He was not very often on time. → ✓ He usually came late.
Word-level: not honest → dishonest; did not remember → forgot; did not pay any
attention to → ignored. Use *not* for denial or antithesis, never evasion.
(Frequently exempted: hedging is a load-bearing trait in many people's voices.)

**Rule 12. Use definite, specific, concrete language.** *(voice-adjacent)*
✗ A period of unfavorable weather set in. → ✓ It rained every day for a week.
Prefer the specific to the general, the concrete to the abstract; in argument,
give particular instances of general principles.

**Rule 13. Omit needless words.** *(voice-adjacent)*
✗ His story is a strange one. → ✓ His story is strange.
✗ the question as to whether → ✓ whether
✗ owing to the fact that → ✓ because
"Make every word tell" — see the appendix for the full pattern list. Also
combine step-by-step clause chains into one sentence when the steps express a
single idea.

**Rule 14. Avoid a succession of loose sentences.** *(voice-adjacent)*
A paragraph of two-clause sentences strung with *and*, *but*, *so*, *which*
turns mechanical and sing-song. Recast some: simple sentences, semicolons,
periodic sentences — whichever matches the real relation of the thoughts.

**Rule 15. Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form.** *(voice-adjacent)*
✗ It was both a long ceremony and very tedious.
✓ The ceremony was both long and tedious.
Correlatives (*both/and*, *not only/but also*, *either/or*, *first/second*)
take the same grammatical construction on each side. Repeat an article or
preposition before every term of a series, or only before the first — not a
mix.

**Rule 16. Keep related words together.**
Subject and verb shouldn't be split by a movable phrase; modifiers sit next to
what they modify; the relative pronoun follows its antecedent.
✗ He only found two mistakes. → ✓ He found only two mistakes.
✗ All the members were not present. → ✓ Not all the members were present.

**Rule 17. In summaries, keep to one tense.**
Present tense for summarizing works; antecedent action in the perfect. Don't
shift tenses mid-summary, and don't intercalate "he said," "the author also
thinks" — mark it as summary once, then proceed.

**Rule 18. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.** *(voice-adjacent)*
✗ This steel is principally used for making razors, because of its hardness.
✓ Because of its hardness, this steel is principally used in making razors.
The end position belongs to the new element — the logical predicate. The
beginning is the second-strongest seat. The same principle scales to sentences
in a paragraph and paragraphs in a composition.

## Appendix A: needless-word patterns (Rule 13 expansion)

| Cut this | Write this |
| --- | --- |
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | no doubt / doubtless |
| used for fuel purposes | used for fuel |
| he is a man who | he |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject which | this subject |
| owing to the fact that | since / because |
| in spite of the fact that | though / although |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you / notify you |
| I was unaware of the fact that | I did not know |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure |
| who is / which was (superfluous) | (drop it: "his brother, a member of the same firm") |

## Appendix B: watch-words (from Section V of the original)

Words the original flags as "constantly obtruding themselves into places where
they have no right to be": *case*, *factor*, *feature*, *interesting*, *one of
the most*, *very*, *as to whether*, *along these lines*. When one appears in a
draft, the fix is usually to recast the sentence, not to swap the word.
